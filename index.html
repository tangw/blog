An Introduction to Cryptarithms

Posted on 2024-01-20 In mathematics

Cryptarithm, also known as alphametic, is a mathematical puzzle where the digits of numbers in a mathematical equation are replaced by letters of the alphabet. The mathematical equation in a cryptarithm is commonly a basic arithmetic calculation such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, or division. The objective is to recover the original equation by identifying the value of each letter that satisfies the equation.

In a standard cryptarithm, each letter must represent a unique digit whenever it occurs in the equation, and the leading digit must not equal zero unless stated otherwise. A good puzzle should have a unique solution, and the letters should make up a phrase. For instance, one of the most famous cryptarithms, created by Henry Dudeney in 1924[1], is given below:

\[\begin{matrix} & & \text{S} & \text{E} & \text{N} & \text{D} \\ + & & \text{M} & \text{O} & \text{R} & \text{E} \\ \hline & \text{M} & \text{O} & \text{N} & \text{E} & \text{Y} \\ \end{matrix}\]

Solving a cryptarithm puzzle by hand usually requires a mix of logical reasoning, simple arithmetic, and exhaustive testing of possibilities. As an example, one of the possible solutions to the above puzzle consists of the following steps:

  1. Number the columns in the equation from low (right) to high (left) such that the single letter M appears in column 5;
  2. From column 5, we know that \(M = 1\) since it is the only carry-over possible from the sum of two single digits in column 4;
  3. In column 4, since S + M generates a carry, O must be less than M. Therefore, \(O = 0\);
  4. Since \(O = 0\), either \(S = 8\) or \(S = 9\), depending on whether there is a carry from column 3. If there was a carry from column 3, N must be less than zero, which is impossible since \(O = 0\). Therefore, there is no carry from column 3, and hence \(S = 9\);
  5. In column 3, E must not equal N because they represent different digits. Since \(O = 0\), there must be a carry from column 2. So \(E + 1 = N\);
  6. In column 2, if there was no carry from column 1, \(N + R = 10 + E\). From step 5, we know that \(E + 1 = N\). So we have \(E + 1 + R = 10 + E\), or \(R = 9\), which is impossible since \(S = 9\). Therefore, there must be a carry from column 1. So \(R = 8\);
  7. To generate a carry from column 1, we must have \(D + E = 10 + Y\);
  8. Since \(y \ne 0\) and \(y \ne 1\), Y is at least two, and D + E is at least 12. The possible pair of choice could be either \((5, 7)\) or \((6, 7)\). So either D or E equals seven. If \(E = 7\), since \(E + 1 = N\), \(N = 8\), which is impossible. So \(D = 7\);
  9. Since \(D = 7\), E cannot be six because \(N = E + 1\) would be seven, which is impossible. Therefore, \(E = 5\) and \(N = 6\);
  10. Finally, from \(D + E = Y + 10\), we know that \(Y = 2\).
  1. 1.H. E. Dudeney, in Strand Magazine vol. 68 (July 1924), pp. 97 and 214. ↩︎
